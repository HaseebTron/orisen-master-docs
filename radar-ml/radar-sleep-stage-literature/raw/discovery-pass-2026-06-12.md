## Scope and screening

This pass focused on **radar-based** work only and was optimized for **high recall**, not full technical extraction. I included sources when the discovery page, abstract, or snippet explicitly tied a radar signal to **sleep-stage classification, sleep-state estimation, REM/NREM labeling, or sleep/wake classification**. I deprioritized pure vital-sign or sleep-apnea papers unless the same source also included sleep labels. I also separated out adjacent radar papers and common false positives so the later extraction pass can stay focused.

At a high level, the literature looks broader than it first appears. This pass surfaced a continuous line from **2015 foundational conference papers** using multiple radar sensors, bioradiolocation, and short-range Doppler systems, through **2018 CW/IR-UWB studies**, into a much denser **2020–2026 cluster** using IR-UWB, UWB, 60–61 GHz FMCW/mmWave, bedside nearables, a smartphone UWB implementation, transfer learning, pediatric cohorts, neonatal cohorts, older-adult cohorts, and product-style devices validated against PSG. The strongest clusters appear around the **Somnofy/Norway line**, the **Seoul National / Korean IR-UWB and 60 GHz line**, **pediatric and preterm UWB studies**, and **newer transfer-learning / product-validation work**. citeturn24search0turn5view1turn5view4turn6view0turn6view2turn9search0turn19search1turn31search0turn39search6turn8view2turn7view1

A second pattern is methodological. Older and mid-period papers often use **derived movement, respiration, heartbeat, or HRV-like features from radar** and then map them to sleep labels. Recent work increasingly uses **deep learning, transfer learning, domain adaptation, larger PSG-synchronized cohorts, and consumer-facing hardware formats** such as a bedside lamp, Nest Hub-style bedside radar, or a smartphone with UWB radar. citeturn5view1turn5view4turn9search0turn6view2turn19search0turn7view1turn8view2turn21view0

## Open questions and limitations

This was intentionally a **discovery-only** pass, so I did not extract architectures, feature sets, label definitions, performance tables, subject counts beyond what was obvious, or dataset details exhaustively. Some sources—especially conference abstracts, poster listings, and certain publisher pages—were only available at **snippet level** in this pass, so several rows are marked **maybe** even though they look promising.

The most important ambiguity to keep in mind is the boundary between **radar** and broader **RF/radio** sensing. I screened out high-profile non-radar or unclear-radio sources when the discovery evidence did not clearly establish radar use, but a later pass could still revisit those decisions if a source’s full text proves it used a true radar formulation.
