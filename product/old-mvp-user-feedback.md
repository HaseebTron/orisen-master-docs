## Summary

Founder reports that the old MVP testers expressed that the device was much better at helping them wake up on time compared with their normal wake-up behavior.

The strongest qualitative signal was not that the old MVP made waking pleasant. The strongest signal was that it made wake-up completion much harder to avoid.

Testers also expressed interest in the future artificial sleep phase transitioning / easier wake-up direction because the old MVP could wake them and force them out of bed, but could still leave them feeling groggy or affected by sleep inertia.

## Main qualitative feedback themes

### Theme 1: Much better at waking up on time than before

Founder-reported feedback:

- Testers expressed that the old MVP was much better at waking them up on time compared with their previous/default wake-up behavior.
- The feedback matched the table-level evidence where testers often got out of bed closer to alarm time than their usual baseline.

Interpretation:

- This strengthens the wake-completion wedge.
- The first customer value is reliable wake completion, not general sleep optimization.
- The product should continue to focus on the painful gap between alarm time and actually leaving bed.

Claim boundary:

- Supports: early qualitative validation of wake-completion value.
- Does not prove: broad market demand, long-term retention, production reliability, or willingness to pay across cold customers.

### Theme 2: Dennis said he would buy it right now

Founder-reported feedback:

- Dennis expressed that he would buy the device “right now” during the old MVP testing period.
- Founder believes the mentioned price at the time may have been about $100.

Interpretation:

- This is an early willingness-to-pay signal from one tester.
- It is stronger than a generic compliment, because it suggests purchase intent.
- It is still weak as market evidence because it came from one friend/tester and no actual purchase/preorder was completed.

Claim boundary:

- Supports: one early tester expressed purchase intent, likely around a roughly $100 discussed price point.
- Does not prove: validated price point, broad willingness to pay, repeatable sales, preorder demand, or product-market fit.

### Theme 3: Testers wanted easier wake-up / artificial sleep phase transitioning

Founder-reported feedback:

- Founder had mentioned the artificial sleep phase transitioning concept and easier wake-up direction to testers.
- Testers expressed interest in that feature.
- Dennis especially wanted that direction.
- The reason was that the old MVP helped wake users and get them out of bed, but could still leave them groggy or affected by sleep inertia.

Interpretation:

- This supports the roadmap logic: wake completion is the first wedge, while grogginess reduction/easier wake-up is the next major value expansion.
- It suggests that forced wake completion alone solves an urgent problem but does not solve the full morning experience.
- Users may still want Orisen to make waking feel less brutal, not only make waking unavoidable.

Claim boundary:

- Supports: user pull for a less-groggy/easier wake-up feature direction.
- Does not prove: artificial sleep phase transitioning works, grogginess reduction is validated, sleep-stage detection works, or the product can reliably move users into lighter sleep.

## Product interpretation

The old MVP feedback suggests a two-layer value proposition:

1. Guaranteed wake completion:
   - gets the user up
   - prevents staying in bed
   - prevents going back to bed
   - makes wake-up completion difficult to avoid

2. Easier wake-up / reduced grogginess:
   - users still want the wake-up to feel less brutal
   - old MVP did not fully solve sleep inertia
   - artificial sleep phase transitioning remains a strategic hypothesis/roadmap direction, not a validated outcome

This supports the current positioning hierarchy:

- Lead with guaranteed wake-up / wake completion.
- Treat easier wake-up / grogginess reduction as an important but claim-sensitive next layer.
- Do not lead with unvalidated sleep-stage manipulation claims as if proven.

## Evidence strength

For wake-completion value:
- Evidence category: early validation / qualitative pilot feedback.
- Evidence strength: Level 2 to early Level 3 when combined with old MVP tables.

For willingness to pay:
- Evidence category: early signal.
- Evidence strength: Level 1 to Level 2.
- Reason: one tester expressed purchase intent, but no transaction or broad price test occurred.

For artificial sleep phase transitioning / easier wake-up demand:
- Evidence category: hypothesis with early user pull.
- Evidence strength: Level 1 to Level 2.
- Reason: testers expressed interest, but technical efficacy was not validated.

## Safe ways to use this evidence

Safe:

- “Early testers said the prototype was much better at getting them up on time than their normal alarms.”
- “One tester said he would buy it immediately, around a roughly $100 discussed price point.”
- “Testers still wanted wake-ups to feel less groggy, which supports our roadmap beyond wake completion.”
- “The old MVP validated the pain: waking up on time is not enough if the user still feels brutal afterward.”

Avoid:

- “Users proved they will pay $100.”
- “Customers validated artificial sleep phase transitioning.”
- “Orisen reduces sleep inertia.”
- “Orisen guarantees easy wake-ups.”
- “The old MVP solved grogginess.”
- “The pilot proved product-market fit.”
