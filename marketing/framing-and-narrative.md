# Framing and Narrative

## Pitch deck narrative

- A pitch deck is a sequence of beliefs you install in the investor’s mind.
  - Each slide should make them think:
    - problem: “Oh… that’s a real problem”
    - market size: “Wait, this is bigger than I thought”
    - “Hmm, current solutions actually suck”
    - “Okay, this approach is different”
    - “Damn, this could be huge”
    - “Why hasn’t this been done already?”
    - “Oh, that’s why now”
    - “And these guys might actually pull it off”
  - By the end: “This feels inevitable. I don’t want to miss this.”

- Good decks don’t feel like persuasion. They feel like **clarity.**

| Weak framing | Strong framing |
| --- | --- |
| “We tell a compelling story” | “We reveal a truth people hadn’t seen clearly before” |
| “We hype the idea” | “We make the idea feel obvious in hindsight” |
| “We sound exciting” | “We feel inevitable” |

- Narratives do need to be backed
  - ground it with facts, numbers, and proofs
  - if you dont, it sounds like: a reach, buzzwords, sci-fi

## Problem and solution narrative movement

- problem: reframe → expose gap
- solution: introduce inevitability → prove early signal

## Instilling a Narrative into someone

You never change someone's mind by attacking their current belief. You change it by first validating their current reality, then introducing a tension they can't resolve within their existing frame, then offering a new frame that resolves the tension cleanly.

### General overview

You’re moving them through:

1. Familiar → “I know this”
2. Friction → “Something’s off”
3. Insight → “Oh wait…”
4. Expansion → “This is big”
5. Replacement → “New way to think”
6. Resolution → “This makes sense”

### Step 1: Start with something undeniable: The current world model

- Open with something they already agree with.
- Validate the current reality(current model)
- get them nodding at something they already believe is true.

### Step 2: Introduce a tension: Gently challenge the norm

- The most important move is the tension. Nobody changes a frame they're comfortable with. You have to make the old frame feel wrong before they'll accept a new one.
- makes the current reality feel unacceptable

### Step 3: Reveal the insight: Offer a new lens that resolves the tension

- Now you drop the reframe, This is the “click” moment.
- offer them a new lens to see the world through.
- Once they accept the reframe, your solution feels inevitable rather than optional.
- Notice also that none of them argue against the old frame directly. Apple never said "PCs are for boring people." De Beers never said "small diamonds mean you love her less." They just built the new frame so compellingly that the old one collapsed on its own

### Step 4: Widen the implication: Show why it matters

- Show why this matters:
  - performance
  - consistency
  - daily life
  - identity
- You want: “This is bigger than I thought”

### Step 5: Destroy current solutions (gently)

Not aggressively. Just logically:

- alarms = brute force
- trackers = passive

Let them conclude: “Yeah… those don’t actually solve it”

### Step 6: Introduce the new model

“Sleep shouldn’t just be tracked. It should be actively guided.”

### Step 7: Present your product: “Oh, this is the thing that fits that model”

- your product should feel like the natural next step
- not “an idea” but “what comes next”
- tie features directly to the new model

### Step 8: Reinforce with proof: So they don’t think this is just a theory

- Without proof, it sounds like philosophy.
- examples:
  - waitlist
  - prototype
  - user reactions
- You’re telling them: “This isn’t just a perspective. It’s already starting.”

### Anchor the new belief: A concise phrase that reminds them of the new world view

- An anchor is a word, phrase, image or number that encodes an entire belief system into something small enough to carry around in your head.
- called an anchor for the same reason a ship's anchor works — it fixes something in place.
- Once an anchor is installed in someone's mind, it holds the new frame steady even when the person isn't actively thinking about it.
- Every time they encounter something related to your space, the anchor pulls them back to your frame automatically.
- for example for the mac, once "Think Different" is in your head, every computer purchase gets evaluated through that lens first whether you want it to or not.
- **Why are anchors so powerful specifically:**
  - First, the brain is lazy. It doesn't re-evaluate every situation from scratch. It looks for a shortcut — a stored frame it can apply quickly. Anchors become that shortcut.
  - Second, anchors are social. "A Diamond is Forever" works partly because everyone knows it. Shared anchors create shared reality. When everyone in a culture uses the same frame, the frame stops feeling like a frame and starts feeling like just the way things are.
  - Third, anchors are emotional not logical. "Think Different" doesn't make a logical argument. It makes you feel something about yourself. Feelings are stickier than facts.

## How to figure out the narrative you want to paint (for problem)

### Step 1: define current belief

- what people already think:
  - alarms work fine
  - oversleeping is discipline
  - sleep tech = tracking (wearables, apps)
  - this is not a big problem
- these are what you need to break

### Step 2: define final belief

- write a single sentence: “after this pitch, the investor should believe: ____”
- examples for Orisen:
  - sleep will become controllable, not just trackable
  - waking up is not solved, current tools are primitive
  - this is a new category, not just a product
  - it is a real and abundant problem

### Step 3: Method 1: Figuring out what the new framing should be

#### Step 1 — Find the thing people already secretly believe but haven't said out loud

- The best frames don't introduce a foreign idea.
  - They articulate something that was already half-formed in people's heads.
- Obama's "Change" worked because people already felt the system was broken.
  - He just gave that feeling a home.
- Uber worked because everyone already thought taxis were terrible.
  - They just assumed it was permanent.
- For Orisen:
  - People already feel like mornings are brutal and they have no control.
  - You're not teaching them that.
  - You're just being the first person to say it clearly and offer them a reason to believe it can change.

#### Step 2 — Find the villain

- Every compelling frame has something to push against.
  - It doesn't have to be a person.
  - It can be a system, an assumption, a technology, an era.
- Apple's villain was conformity.
- Uber's villain was the taxi monopoly.
- Tesla's villain was the assumption that sustainable had to mean compromise.
- Your villain is the assumption that sleep is something that happens to you rather than something you can control.
- The entire sleep industry has been built around that assumption.
  - Trackers, wearables, apps all accept the premise that sleep is beyond intervention.
- Your frame says that premise is wrong.

#### Step 3 — Find the inversion

- The new frame is almost always the direct opposite of the old assumption.
- Taxis are unreliable.
  - Rides should be one tap.
- Hotels are impersonal.
  - Travel should feel like belonging.
- Fossil fuels are the performance standard.
  - Electricity is faster.
- For Orisen, the inversion is:
  - Sleep has always controlled humans.
  - Orisen gives humans control over sleep.
- That's it.
  - That's your frame in one sentence.

#### Step 4 — Pressure test it against the cynical version

- The best founders and strategists ask:
  - What's the most cynical way someone could hear this?
- If the cynical version sounds true, the frame isn't working yet.
- "Manipulates your sleep"
  - Cynical version sounds sinister.
  - Bad frame.
- "Fixes your sleep"
  - Cynical version is "that's a big claim."
  - Needs proof.
- "Gives you control over your sleep"
  - Cynical version is curiosity.
  - Good frame.

#### Step 5 — Compress it until it's almost too small

- The frame has to survive being repeated in a hallway by someone who half-remembers it.
- If it takes a paragraph to explain, it won't spread.
- De Beers had a whole philosophy about love and commitment and permanence.
  - It survived as four words.
- Your frame has to survive the same compression.

### Step 3: Method 2: Figuring out what the new framing should be

#### step 2: identify hidden truth / insight

- how to find:
  - Ask: “What is true that people don’t explicitly realize yet?”
  - This is your **reframe lever:** the *single core insight* that lets you shift someone from their current belief to a new belief
- lets you move someone’s entire mental model with one idea
- This is the core of your narrative.
- examples:
  - humans don’t actually control when they wake up
  - alarms are brute-force, not intelligent
  - current sleep tech is passive, not active
- what makes a good reframe lever
  - true
  - simple
  - slightly surprising
  - hard to unsee once heard
- examples of reframe levers (important)
  - Airbnb
    - lever: “people don’t want hotels, they want to feel like they belong”
    - effect: shifts from “cheaper hotel alternative” → “new way to travel”
  - Uber
    - lever: “transportation isn’t about owning cars, it’s about access to rides”
    - effect: shifts from “taxi app” → “on-demand mobility network”
  - Stripe
    - lever: “payments should be programmable infrastructure, not a banking process”
    - effect: shifts from “payment tool” → “internet infrastructure”
- how to know you’ve found a strong lever
  - test it mentally:
    - does it make current solutions feel dumb?
    - does it make the problem feel bigger?
    - does it open the door to a new category?
    - does it make your product feel like a natural next step?
  - if yes → strong lever
  - if not → it’s just a statement, not a lever

#### step 3: move from symptom → root cause

- symptom: people oversleep
- root cause:
  - no system exists to control waking
  - we can’t control our sleep phases
- “The real problem isn’t oversleeping. It’s that we don’t control the waking process at all.”
- always go one layer deeper

## What looks like a successful narrative

- feels obvious after hearing it
- makes current solutions look outdated
- makes them think, they’ve been doing it wrong this whole time
- creates a new category in the investors head
