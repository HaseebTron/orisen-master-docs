## Purpose

This file defines Orisen's reply strategy for the founder's personal X account.

Replies are treated as a separate growth and discovery workflow from owned posts because early low-follower accounts often need to earn profile visits through existing conversations.

Use this file to decide:

- who to reply to
- what conversations to look for
- what search terms to use
- how to reply without sounding spammy
- when to mention Orisen
- when to invite DMs
- when not to reply
- how replies feed customer discovery, post ideas, and waitlist growth

This file is not:

- a finished reply bank
- a finished post bank
- a customer conversation log
- a performance log
- a claims authority
- a replacement for `marketing/x/playbook.md`
- a replacement for `marketing/x/voice.md`

Finished or near-finished reply drafts belong in `marketing/x/post-bank.md` later.

## Reply goals

Replies should help Orisen grow through useful participation, not through forced promotion.

Use replies to:

- earn profile visits from relevant conversations
- find people with repeated wake-up failure
- learn exact customer language
- create customer-discovery conversations
- build trust with builders, operators, experts, and investors
- make Orisen's worldview familiar without pitching constantly
- test which pain points and reframes resonate in the wild
- create ideas for original posts
- invite qualified users into DMs when natural

Do not use replies mainly to:

- drop links
- pitch Orisen repeatedly
- chase random impressions
- force the product into unrelated threads
- argue with people who are not open to the conversation
- look like a brand account doing engagement tactics

A good reply outcome is not just likes.

A good reply outcome can be:

- a high-pain user shares their exact morning failure mode
- someone asks what Orisen is
- someone DMs asking to try it
- an expert gives useful feedback
- a builder/operator engages with a thoughtful product point
- a reply reveals language worth using in future posts

## Who replies are for

### Primary audience

Prioritize people with repeated, painful wake-up failure.

Look for people who say or imply that they:

- sleep through alarms
- set many alarms
- dismiss alarms while half-asleep
- wake up but stay in bed
- turn alarms off and go back to sleep
- return to bed after initially getting up
- lose time between alarm time and actually being out of bed
- feel that normal alarms and willpower do not work
- face real consequences when they fail to wake up
- want a system that makes wake completion harder to avoid

The strongest filter is behavioral:

> Does this person repeatedly fail between alarm time and actually being out of bed?

Do not spend too much reply effort on casual sleep-optimization users who only want sleep scores, general wellness tips, or a nicer alarm sound.

### Secondary audience

Reply to people who can amplify, advise, invest, or connect Orisen to the right early users.

This includes:

- hardware founders
- consumer tech founders
- health and wellness operators
- sleep-tech operators
- startup builders
- early-stage investors
- product people
- engineers interested in sensing, reliability, behavior change, or consumer hardware

This audience matters, but it should not pull the account into generic startup content.

### Tertiary audience

Reply carefully to sleep experts, clinicians, researchers, and technical reviewers.

Good reasons to reply:

- ask for feedback on validation thinking
- learn from expertise
- clarify careful product direction
- show that Orisen is serious about evidence and claim discipline

Bad reasons to reply:

- promote Orisen in clinical conversations
- debate medical topics loosely
- imply Orisen has proven outcomes it has not validated
- use expert threads as a product pitch surface

## Account types to reply under

Prioritize accounts where the audience or conversation quality is likely to help Orisen.

### Best-fit accounts

Reply under:

- people publicly complaining about painful wake-up failure
- founders building consumer products
- founders building hardware
- founders building health, wellness, sleep, behavior-change, or quantified-self products
- product leaders talking about user behavior
- engineers talking about reliability, sensing, embedded systems, or consumer devices
- credible sleep or behavior experts
- investors discussing consumer, hardware, health, wellness, or AI hardware
- startup operators with thoughtful audiences

### Lower-fit accounts

Reply less often under:

- generic motivational accounts
- generic productivity accounts
- broad viral meme accounts
- random engagement-bait threads
- accounts where every comment section is low-quality
- threads where the only possible reply is a forced Orisen pitch

### Rule of thumb

Before replying, ask:

> Would a useful person care if they clicked through to my profile after this reply?

If the answer is no, skip it.

## Daily reply workflow

Keep the workflow light enough to actually do.

On days when time allows:

1. Spend 10 to 20 minutes searching high-intent terms.
2. Open 5 to 10 relevant threads.
3. Reply only where there is a real angle.
4. Use the basic reply rhythm:
   - reflect the point
   - add one specific thought
   - ask one useful question if natural
5. Avoid links by default.
6. Mention Orisen only when the thread naturally fits.
7. Move to DM only when the person is clearly high-signal.
8. Save useful customer language or post ideas.

Starting target:

- 10 to 20 thoughtful replies per day on days when time allows
- fewer replies is fine if they are higher quality
- do not reply just to hit a number

A good reply should feel like Haseeb joining a conversation.

Not Orisen entering a thread.

## Reply style

Replies should usually be more casual than original posts.

Good reply voice:

- short
- specific
- conversational
- useful
- lightly opinionated
- matched to the energy of the original post
- curious when there is a chance to learn
- human enough that it does not feel scheduled or copied

Avoid reply voice that sounds like:

- a pitch
- a lecture
- a customer-support script
- a brand account
- a copy-pasted engagement tactic
- an AI assistant trying to sound friendly

Default rhythm:

> reflect the point + add one specific thought + ask one useful question if natural

Do not ask a question in every reply.

Sometimes the best reply is just one useful sentence.

## Reply patterns

These are reply patterns, not finished reply drafts.

Do not treat this section as a reply bank.

### Pain reflection

Use when someone describes a real morning failure.

Structure:

1. Mirror the actual pain.
2. Name the failure point more clearly.
3. Stop, or ask one useful question.

Useful when the person says they slept through alarms, turned them off, or lost time after waking.

### Sharp insight

Use when the thread is about alarms, snoozing, or morning failure.

Structure:

1. Agree with or sharpen the thread's point.
2. Add one specific reframe.
3. Keep it short.

Useful for making Orisen's worldview familiar without pitching.

### Customer-discovery question

Use when the person seems like a high-pain user.

Structure:

1. Acknowledge the problem.
2. Ask what usually fails.
3. Keep the question easy to answer.

Good question angles:

- Do they sleep through the alarm?
- Do they wake up and turn it off?
- Do they wake up but stay in bed?
- Do they get up and then return to bed?
- What have they already tried?
- What would feel too extreme?
- What would actually help?

### Builder or product thought

Use when replying to founders, builders, operators, or product people.

Structure:

1. Connect the thread to a real product tradeoff.
2. Say the practical implication.
3. Avoid overexplaining Orisen unless asked.

Good angles:

- designing around half-awake behavior
- local reliability at alarm time
- anti-bypass without trapping the user
- bedroom privacy and no-camera sensing
- behavior completion instead of button taps
- reliability before intelligence

### Expert humility

Use when replying to sleep experts, clinicians, researchers, or technical reviewers.

Structure:

1. Show that the topic is being treated carefully.
2. Ask a specific question or invite correction.
3. Avoid product promotion.

Good angles:

- validation approach
- measuring wake completion
- subjective grogginess tracking
- sleep-stage validation limits
- consumer claim boundaries
- real-world behavior versus lab measurement

### Soft Orisen mention

Use only when Orisen is directly relevant.

Structure:

1. Name the problem in the thread.
2. Mention that this is the problem Orisen is being built around.
3. Keep it brief.
4. Do not link unless asked.

The mention should feel like context, not a pitch.

### DM transition

Use when someone clearly matches the target customer or asks for more.

Structure:

1. Acknowledge the specific failure mode they described.
2. Say why it is relevant to what you are learning or building.
3. Invite DM for a short conversation.

The DM invite should feel human and specific.

Not automated.

## When to mention Orisen

Mention Orisen when:

- someone directly asks what you are building
- the thread is specifically about alarm failure, oversleeping, or wake completion
- the person describes the exact problem Orisen is built for
- the product context would make the reply more useful
- someone asks for solutions or products
- someone asks for the waitlist
- you are inviting a high-pain user to give feedback or join the waitlist

Do not mention Orisen when:

- the thread is only loosely related
- the person is venting and a product mention would feel gross
- the topic is clinical, medical, or sensitive
- the reply would require a long product explanation
- the mention would sound like a brand account forcing itself into the thread
- the reply is better as a simple observation or question

Default rule:

> Earn the right to mention Orisen by first adding something useful.

## DM transition rules

DMs are for high-signal conversations, not cold pitching at scale.

Move to DM when:

- someone says they repeatedly oversleep
- someone sets many alarms and still fails
- someone says they turn alarms off half-asleep
- someone wakes up but cannot get out of bed
- someone gets up and returns to bed
- someone asks to try Orisen
- someone asks for the waitlist
- someone gives expert or operator feedback worth continuing privately
- someone appears like a strong early tester or interview candidate

Do not move to DM when:

- the person gave only a low-signal joke reply
- the thread is unrelated to Orisen's target problem
- the conversation is clinical or sensitive
- the DM would feel like a sales pitch
- the person has not shown enough relevance

A good DM flow:

1. Mention the specific thing they said.
2. Ask one simple question about their wake-up failure mode.
3. Learn first.
4. Share Orisen or the waitlist only after relevance is clear or requested.

Good DM goal:

> Turn a high-signal public reply into a real customer-discovery conversation.

Bad DM goal:

> Send as many people as possible the same pitch.

## Link rules

Do not link-drop by default.

A link in a reply should feel earned.

Use links when:

- someone asks what Orisen is
- someone asks for the waitlist
- someone asks where to follow the product
- the thread is already about Orisen
- the conversation has clearly moved from discovery to intent

Avoid links when:

- the reply would be stronger as a question
- the person is venting
- the thread is only loosely related
- the account is small and the link would make the reply feel spammy
- the product has not been explained enough for the link to make sense

Default path:

1. Reply usefully.
2. Continue if they engage.
3. Move to DM if high-signal.
4. Share the link when requested or clearly relevant.

## What not to reply to

Avoid replying when:

- the topic is clinical sleep-disorder treatment
- the person appears to need medical support, not a consumer product
- the conversation is about severe health distress
- the only possible reply is a pitch
- the thread is rage bait
- the reply would mock people who struggle to wake up
- the reply would make Orisen sound like a punishment device
- the reply would make Orisen sound impossible to override
- the thread would force Orisen into unsupported sleep-stage or clinical claims
- the account is low-quality engagement bait
- the conversation is political or unrelated drama
- the comment section is full of bad-faith replies

Do not shame users.

Target the broken system, not the person struggling with mornings.
