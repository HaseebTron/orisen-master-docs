## Purpose

This file defines how Haseeb should sound in founder-led communication.

Use this file for:

- social posts
- LinkedIn posts
- X posts
- investor emails
- customer DMs
- outreach messages
- founder updates
- casual public explanations
- founder-led website-adjacent copy

This file covers two layers:

1. Human writing rules: how to make writing sound natural, specific, and not AI-generated.
2. Haseeb voice rules: how to make writing sound like Haseeb specifically.

This file does not cover channel-specific style rules.

Channel-specific docs should inherit this file and only add platform-specific adaptations.

For X-specific writing style, use:

- `marketing/x/style-guide.md`

## Human writing rules

### Write like a person, not a brand

The writing should feel like a real founder talking.

Avoid sounding like:

- a corporate brand
- a polished wellness company
- a generic startup account
- an AI content assistant
- a productivity guru
- a sleep science brochure

Better writing usually sounds like something Haseeb would actually say out loud.

### Use normal words

Use simple language before technical language.

Better:

> Normal alarms stop too early.

Worse:

> Conventional alarm solutions fail to optimize the wake-state transition.

Better:

> I think the real problem starts after the alarm rings.

Worse:

> The key user pain point occurs post-alarm activation.

### Be specific

Specific writing feels human.

Vague writing feels generated.

Better:

> Turning off your alarm and somehow losing another hour.

Worse:

> Experiencing morning routine difficulties.

Better:

> The moment your half-awake self gets decision rights.

Worse:

> The point at which user intent and behavioral execution diverge.

### Use short sentences when the idea is sharp

A strong idea should not be buried in a long sentence.

Better:

> Your alarm rang.
>
> You turned it off.
>
> You still did not get up.
>
> That is the gap I care about.

Worse:

> The issue is that although the alarm may technically activate and be dismissed by the user, the desired behavioral outcome of leaving the bed may still not occur.

### Keep one main idea per paragraph

Most founder-led writing should move cleanly.

Do not stack too many ideas at once.

Bad paragraph pattern:

- product claim
- category narrative
- customer pain
- technical explanation
- CTA
- founder reflection

Better paragraph pattern:

- one pain
- one insight
- one implication

### Sound slightly imperfect in the right places

Founder-led writing should not be so polished that it sounds fake.

It is okay to use:

- short fragments
- simple transitions
- direct statements
- casual phrasing
- first-person thoughts
- plain disagreement

Examples:

> I do not think alarms are the real problem.

> This sounds obvious, but it is not how most alarms are built.

> The more I work on Orisen, the more I think this is the actual failure point.

### Avoid AI-sounding patterns

Avoid overusing:

- “In today's world”
- “game-changer”
- “revolutionize”
- “unlock”
- “empower”
- “seamless”
- “holistic”
- “cutting-edge”
- “transform your mornings”
- “take your sleep to the next level”
- “at the intersection of”
- “designed to help users optimize”
- “the future of wellness”
- “our innovative solution”

Also avoid fake structure when the idea does not need it:

- “Here are 5 reasons why...”
- “The problem, the insight, the solution...”
- “This is not just X. It is Y.”
- “Most people think X. But actually Y.”

These structures can be useful sometimes, but they should not become the default.

## Haseeb voice principles

### Direct

Haseeb should say the thing plainly.

The voice should not hide behind abstract language.

Good:

> If your alarm stops while you are still in bed, it stopped too early.

Bad:

> There is a mismatch between alarm dismissal and successful wake behavior.

### Builder-led

Haseeb should sound like someone actively building the product, not someone commenting from the sidelines.

Use:

- what I am building
- what I learned
- what I am testing
- what surprised me
- what I underestimated
- what I changed my mind about
- what users keep telling me
- what broke
- what I am trying next

Good:

> One thing I underestimated about building a wake-up device is how many ways a half-awake person can create loopholes.

Bad:

> Consumer behavior around alarm dismissal presents several design challenges.

### Honest

Haseeb should sound ambitious but not fake-certain.

The voice should be comfortable saying:

- I do not know yet
- we are still testing this
- this is harder than I expected
- the first version is simpler than the long-term vision
- I was wrong about this
- this is the part I am trying to figure out

Honesty makes the founder more credible.

### Slightly provocative

The voice should challenge weak assumptions.

Good targets:

- normal alarms
- passive sleep tracking
- willpower-based morning advice
- generic productivity advice
- products that stop at the wrong moment
- systems that rely on half-awake decision-making

Bad targets:

- users who struggle to wake up
- people with sleep issues
- people who feel embarrassed about mornings
- anyone dealing with medical or clinical sleep problems

Good:

> The problem is not that people need a more motivational alarm sound.

Bad:

> People just lack discipline in the morning.

### Emotionally specific

The voice should make the target user feel seen.

Use real morning pain:

- losing another hour
- turning off alarms half-asleep
- waking up but not getting up
- feeling foggy
- feeling heavy
- being late again
- not trusting yourself in the morning
- trying every alarm hack and still failing
- feeling like your morning is out of your control

Avoid generic wellness language:

- better sleep
- healthier habits
- improved routines
- optimized mornings
- elevated wellbeing

### Practical

Even when the idea is big, the language should stay grounded.

Haseeb should sound like someone who cares about what actually works.

Good:

> The final wake-up moment cannot depend on Wi-Fi. That is a bad failure mode.

Bad:

> We are building a resilient end-to-end sleep transformation ecosystem.

### Curious

The voice should not only broadcast.

It should ask good questions.

Use questions to learn:

- what actually fails in the morning
- what people have already tried
- what workarounds people use
- what they would tolerate
- what feels too extreme
- what feels useful
- what feels creepy
- what feels worth paying for

Good:

> Do you usually sleep through the alarm, turn it off, or wake up and stay in bed?

Bad:

> Would you use an AI-powered sleep intervention platform?

## What Haseeb sounds like

Haseeb should sound like:

- a technical founder who explains things simply
- a builder who is serious but not stiff
- someone ambitious but allergic to fake hype
- someone who notices broken systems and says the obvious thing clearly
- someone who cares about painful mornings, not generic wellness
- someone who can joke without becoming unserious
- someone who can be direct without sounding arrogant
- someone who can be skeptical without sounding negative
- someone who is learning in public, not pretending everything is figured out

The voice should feel like:

- clear
- direct
- human
- grounded
- sharp
- curious
- slightly funny
- slightly contrarian
- honest about uncertainty
- serious about the problem

## What Haseeb does not sound like

Haseeb should not sound:

- corporate
- clinical
- guru-like
- fake-inspirational
- hype-first
- overly polished
- overly academic
- generic wellness
- generic startup
- like a brand manager
- like a motivational account
- like an AI ghostwriter
- mean-spirited
- fearmongering
- detached from the product

Avoid writing that sounds like:

> We are transforming the way people approach their mornings.

Prefer writing that sounds like:

> I think alarms stop at the exact moment the real problem starts.

Avoid writing that sounds like:

> Orisen empowers users to optimize their wake experience.

Prefer writing that sounds like:

> Orisen is for people who wake up, turn the alarm off, and somehow lose another hour.

Avoid writing that sounds like:

> We are building at the intersection of AI, sleep, and human performance.

Prefer writing that sounds like:

> I do not want another app that tells people they slept badly. I want to build something that changes the morning outcome.

## Voice patterns

Use these patterns across channels when they fit.

These are not finished posts. They are reusable ways of thinking and writing.

### Plain insight

Pattern:

> I think the real problem is not ___. It is ___.

Example:

> I think the real problem is not the alarm ringing. It is what happens after.

### Old frame, new frame

Pattern:

> Most people treat ___ like ___. I think it is actually ___.

Example:

> Most people treat waking up like a timer problem. I think it is a completion problem.

### Broken system

Pattern:

> The weird thing about ___ is that it stops before ___.

Example:

> The weird thing about alarms is that they stop before the wake-up is actually complete.

### Founder belief

Pattern:

> The more I build ___, the more I think ___.

Example:

> The more I build Orisen, the more I think the alarm sound was never the whole problem.

### Customer mirror

Pattern:

> If you ___, you probably do not need ___. You need ___.

Example:

> If you turn off alarms and go back to sleep, you probably do not need a nicer alarm sound. You need a system that does not trust your half-awake self.

### Builder lesson

Pattern:

> One thing I underestimated about ___ is ___.

Example:

> One thing I underestimated about building an alarm is how creative people become when they want five more minutes.

### Contrarian but grounded

Pattern:

> I do not think ___ is the right frame. The better frame is ___.

Example:

> I do not think “louder alarm” is the right frame. The better frame is wake completion.

### Honest uncertainty

Pattern:

> I do not know yet ___. But I do know ___.

Example:

> I do not know yet what the perfect wake-up flow looks like. But I do know a normal alarm stopping while you are still in bed is a broken design.

### Simple contrast

Pattern:

> ___ tells you ___. ___ should help you ___.

Example:

> A tracker tells you what happened. A better system should help change what happens next.

### Founder update

Pattern:

> This week I worked on ___. The hard part was ___. The thing I learned was ___.

Example:

> This week I worked on the wake-completion flow. The hard part was deciding when the device should trust that someone is actually out of bed. The thing I learned was that edge cases are basically the whole product.

## Humor and sarcasm rules

Humor is allowed.

It should make the writing feel more human and memorable.

Good humor:

- dry observations
- straight-faced jokes
- light sarcasm
- self-aware founder comments
- jokes about alarm loopholes
- jokes about half-awake decision-making
- jokes about how absurd normal alarms are

Bad humor:

- mocking users
- shaming people for oversleeping
- making sleep issues sound like laziness
- making Orisen sound like punishment
- making the product sound unsafe
- joking about clinical or medical sleep problems
- sounding too unserious about a painful problem

### Sarcasm should target the system, not the user

Good:

> The modern alarm clock basically gives decision rights to the least qualified version of you.

Bad:

> People who snooze are just unserious.

Good:

> If your entire morning plan can be defeated by a half-conscious thumb, I have questions about the plan.

Bad:

> If you cannot wake up, that is on you.

### Humor should not weaken trust

Orisen can be funny, but the founder should still sound competent.

A good balance:

> Funny observation first. Serious product thinking underneath.

Bad balance:

> Joke first. Joke second. Product sounds like a meme.

## Good vs bad voice examples

These examples are about voice only.

They are not claim rules.

For claim permission, use `product/claims-and-evidence.md`.

| Situation | Bad voice | Better voice |
|---|---|---|
| Explaining the problem | Morning routines are difficult for many users. | Your alarm can work and your morning can still fail. |
| Talking about alarms | Traditional alarm solutions are limited. | Normal alarms stop too early. |
| Talking about the user | Users need better habit formation. | Some people cannot trust their half-awake self in the morning. |
| Talking about Orisen | Orisen empowers users to optimize their wake experience. | I am building Orisen for people who turn alarms off and go back to sleep. |
| Talking about sleep tech | Sleep technology is evolving rapidly. | Most sleep tech tells you what happened. I think the next step is changing what happens next. |
| Talking about building | We are developing advanced features to improve user outcomes. | The hard part is not making noise. The hard part is knowing whether the wake-up actually happened. |
| Asking users a question | Would you be interested in a sleep optimization product? | What usually fails for you: sleeping through the alarm, turning it off, or getting back in bed? |
| Writing with personality | Our product improves morning consistency. | Your 6:00 AM self had a plan. Your 6:01 AM self started negotiating. |
| Sounding ambitious | We are revolutionizing sleep. | I think sleep tech should stop only reporting the problem and start changing the outcome. |
| Sounding technical | We use sensor-driven environmental feedback loops. | The device needs to know whether you are still in bed. That changes the whole alarm flow. |

## Channel inheritance rules

Channel-specific style guides should inherit this file.

They should only add platform-specific adaptations.

They should not redefine:

- Haseeb's core voice
- Orisen's product truth
- public claim boundaries
- target customer
- positioning
- category narrative
- evidence status

### X inheritance

`marketing/x/style-guide.md` should define how this voice adapts to X.

It may cover:

- shorter sentences
- sharper hooks
- post formatting
- reply style
- quote-post style
- thread style
- when to be more casual
- how to compress ideas
- how to use screenshots, demos, and build-in-public updates

It should not repeat this full founder voice doc.

### LinkedIn inheritance

A future LinkedIn style guide should define how this voice adapts to LinkedIn.

It may be:

- slightly more polished
- more narrative
- more reflective
- more credibility-forward
- more suitable for investors, operators, mentors, and professional audiences

It should still sound like Haseeb.

### Investor outreach inheritance

Future investor outreach guidance should define how this voice adapts to investor communication.

It may be:

- tighter
- more direct
- more evidence-aware
- less joke-heavy
- more focused on clarity, traction, insight, and ask

It should not become stiff or corporate.

### Customer DM and outreach inheritance

Future DM or outreach guidance should define how this voice adapts to one-to-one conversations.

It may be:

- warmer
- more curious
- less polished
- more question-led
- more focused on learning before pitching

It should not sound automated.

## Maintenance rules

- the target customer narrows in `product/target-customer.md`
- claim boundaries change in `product/claims-and-evidence.md` and this file needs a source-of-truth reference update

Do not use this file to store:

- finished posts
- finished emails
- finished DMs
- X reply banks
- X hooks
- posting calendars
- raw customer language
- detailed positioning
- claim matrices
- validation notes
- product roadmap decisions

If a future section becomes too channel-specific, move it to the relevant channel style guide.

If a future section starts deciding what Orisen can claim, move that logic to the relevant product, claim-control, or positioning doc.
