## Messaging philosophy

Orisen should not sound like a generic wellness gadget.

The language should be:

- direct
- pain-led
- founder-led
- emotionally specific
- slightly provocative
- simple enough for normal people
- ambitious without sounding fake
- clear that Orisen is an intervention system, not a passive tracker

Marketing can be bold about:

- the problem
- the enemy
- the ambition
- the product direction
- the founder's belief
- what Orisen is being built toward

Marketing should be careful with:

- guaranteed outcomes
- sleep-stage control
- sleep inertia claims
- grogginess reduction
- clinical or medical language
- accuracy claims
- production reliability claims
- broad market proof

Core rule:

> Stretch the emotion. Do not stretch the factual state.

## Core positioning

Primary positioning:

> Orisen is a non-contact wake-up system for people who cannot trust themselves to get out of bed after their alarm goes off.

Alternative positioning:

> Orisen is a sleep intervention device that starts by solving the gap between your alarm ringing and you actually leaving bed.

Short positioning:

> Orisen helps turn waking up from a willpower problem into a system.

Category position:

> Not a sleep tracker. Not another alarm app. A wake-completion system.

## Simple explanations

### Plain explanation

A normal alarm stops when you tap a button.

Orisen checks whether you actually got out of bed.

### Slightly stronger explanation

Normal alarms trust your half-awake self.

Orisen does not.

It uses non-contact sensing to detect whether you are still in bed, then keeps the wake-up flow going until you actually complete the wake-up.

### Longer explanation

Orisen is built for people who set alarms, wake up, turn them off, and somehow lose another hour.

The first layer is wake completion: the alarm does not fully stop just because you tapped a button. Orisen uses presence detection to check whether you actually left bed, and it can re-trigger if you return.

The bigger vision is a room-based sleep intervention system that uses sensing, audio, light, and feedback to make waking up feel less brutal over time.

## Main messaging wedge

Lead with this idea:

> The problem is not setting an alarm. The problem is completing the wake-up.

Supporting ideas:

- Waking up is not the same as getting out of bed.
- Dismissing an alarm is not wake completion.
- Your alarm can work perfectly and your morning can still fail.
- Normal alarms stop at the exact moment the real battle starts.
- The dangerous version of you is not awake-you. It is half-awake-you.
- Orisen is built for the gap between hearing the alarm and actually being up.

- Getting out of bed feels like a mission.

Do not speak first to people who only want:

- wake-completion system

- checks whether you actually got out of bed

- designed to protect your morning from your half-awake self
- local alarm execution
- re-trigger if you return to bed

- being built toward less brutal mornings

Use these phrases less often or with care:

- sleep-stage-aware wake intervention
- artificial sleep phase transitioning

- radar and AI-guided intervention
- closed-loop sleep intervention

Avoid leading with technical language before the pain is clear.

Bad first sentence:

> Orisen uses mmWave radar and machine learning for sensor-informed sleep intervention.

Better first sentence:

> Orisen is for people who turn off alarms and go back to sleep.

## Claim-level framework

Use four levels of language.

### Level 1: Current product identity and safe claims

Use when describing what Orisen is and the first validated wedge.

Examples:

- Orisen is a non-contact sleep intervention device for people who struggle to wake up.
- Orisen is built around wake completion, not just alarm playback.
- Orisen uses presence detection to help determine whether you actually got out of bed.
- Orisen is not primarily a sleep tracker.
- Orisen is not a wearable.

### Level 2: Designed-to claims

Use when describing the intended product behavior without promising perfect outcomes.

Examples:

- Designed to make waking up harder to avoid.
- Designed to make going back to bed harder.
- Designed so the easiest path is getting out of bed.
- Designed to help protect your morning from your half-awake self.
- Designed to make wake-up reliability depend on bed exit, not just button taps.

### Level 3: Goal and ambition language

Use when selling the mission, roadmap, and product direction.

Examples:

- Our goal is to make groggy mornings feel like a design flaw, not a personality flaw.
- We are building toward less brutal wake-ups.
- We are going after the sleep-to-wake transition, not just the alarm sound.
- The first goal is reliable wake completion. The bigger goal is making wake-ups feel less brutal.
- We want waking up to feel engineered, not endured.

### Level 4: Long-term vision language

Use when clearly framed as future direction.

Examples:

- The long-term vision is a room-based sleep intervention system that senses, responds, and helps guide the sleep-to-wake transition.
- We are building toward radar and AI-guided wake intervention.
- We want Orisen to eventually prepare your body for waking before the alarm has to fight you.
- The future version of Orisen should not just ring. It should guide you out of sleep.
- Over time, Orisen is being built toward sensor-informed intervention that makes wake-ups smoother, not just louder.

Do not present Level 4 language as a current proven product outcome.

## Vision language

Use vision language when selling the bigger product direction.

Good vision lines:

- We are building toward a future where your room helps guide you out of sleep before your alarm has to fight you.
- The long-term goal is to make brutal wake-ups obsolete.
- We want to turn the sleep-to-wake transition into something designed, adaptive, and guided.
- Orisen starts with wake completion. Over time, it should become a closed-loop sleep intervention system.
- The vision is not better tracking. The vision is changing the outcome.

Use these with clear future framing:

- being built toward
- working toward
- long-term vision
- the goal is
- future versions
- over time
- eventually
- exploring

Avoid wording that sounds like the future outcome is already guaranteed.

## Messaging hierarchy

Lead in this order:

1. Pain: normal alarms fail because they stop before wake completion.
2. Wedge: Orisen checks whether the user actually left bed.
3. Difference: non-contact, no wearable, not a passive tracker.
4. Behavior: alarm can continue or re-trigger if the user returns to bed.
5. Direction: gradual audio/light and future sensor-informed wake intervention.
6. Vision: guide the sleep-to-wake transition and make mornings feel less brutal.

Do not lead with:

- radar technical details
- AI claims
- sleep-stage manipulation
- quantified research stats
- market size
- medical or clinical language

## Evidence-aware language rules

### Customer language

Customer interview language can inspire punchy copy.

Good use:

- Gets you out of bed.
- Need more than another phone alarm.
- Built for people who set too many alarms.
- Built for people who wake up but do not get up.

Careful use:

- “Wakes you up 100% of the time” appeared as ideal customer language, but should not be reused as a claim.

### Old MVP language

Safe use:

- An early prototype demonstrated the core wake-completion loop.
- Early testers perceived meaningful value in being forced or strongly pushed out of bed.
- Early testing suggested users still wanted wake-ups to feel less brutal.

Avoid:

- The old MVP proved Orisen works.
- Users proved they will pay.
- The old MVP solved grogginess.

### External research language

Safe use:

- External waking-up research supports the problem framing around snoozing, delayed bed exit, oversleeping, and missed obligations.
- Research helps explain why waking up is not always a clean one-step event.

Avoid:

- Research proves Orisen works.
- Survey stats prove buyer demand.
- Snoozing is always harmful.

### Traction language

Safe use:

- Early founder-led marketing showed message resonance around the wake-up problem.
- A first organic post generated early waitlist interest.

Avoid:

- We have proven product-market fit.
- The market is validated.
- People have proven they will buy.
