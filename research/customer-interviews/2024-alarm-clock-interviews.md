## Source

Source:

- Founder-conducted customer interviews in summer 2024.
- Founder reports that interviews were done on campus for younger respondents and in a mall for random respondents.
- Founder reports trying to follow The Mom Test style: asking questions and writing answers down without intentionally steering the respondent.
- Attached raw data file: `research/customer-interviews/raw/2024-alarm-clock-responses.csv`.

## Evidence weighting rule

This interview set should be weighted as small-sample qualitative discovery, not statistically valid segmentation.

Important:

- Do not infer valid conclusions by occupation, gender, ethnicity, religion, age group, student status, full-time work status, or living situation.
- There are not enough interviews within any subgroup to support subgroup-level conclusions.
- The data can reveal possible pain patterns and useful customer language.
- The data can support hypotheses about likely ICP traits.
- The data cannot validate the size, strength, or purchase behavior of any segment.
- Treat counts as directional context only, not as statistically meaningful rates.

Correct use:

- “Some interviewees described repeated wake-up or get-out-of-bed problems.”
- “Several responses suggest getting out of bed can be a separate pain from waking up.”
- “Camera discomfort appeared multiple times in this small sample.”

Incorrect use:

- “Students are the ICP because most respondents were students.”
- “Men care more about this than women.”
- “Muslim users are more likely to have the problem.”
- “Full-time workers have weaker/stronger demand than students.”
- “70% of the market is uncomfortable with cameras.”

## Sample

- Total responses: 10.
- Younger respondents: mostly university-age / early-20s participants.
- Age range in data: 19 to 80.
- 8 of 10 respondents were age 23 or younger.
- Gender split in data: 8 male, 2 female.
- Working status in data:
  - 5 university students.
  - 1 university student with part-time work.
  - 2 full-time workers.
  - 2 blank/unknown.

Important limitation:

- This is a small convenience sample.
- It should not be treated as statistically representative.
- It should not be used to make subgroup-level claims.
- It is still useful because it captured direct customer language before the current Orisen positioning existed.

## High-level finding

The interviews directionally support the same broad pattern that later Orisen evidence points toward:

- The best early customer is probably not a generic alarm-clock user.
- A stronger early customer hypothesis is someone with repeated wake-up failure, especially around getting out of bed, heavy sleeping, phone distraction, bad habits, low motivation, or not enough sleep.
- Some people who wake up fine do not care much and are probably not the first customer.
- Camera-based sensing appeared uncomfortable to multiple respondents, which directionally supports Orisen's non-camera direction.

Do not treat these as statistically proven market conclusions.

## Problem intensity split

Question:

- `Are there issues/problems you have while waking up, that you would want to fix?`

Observed responses:

| Response type | Count |
|---|---:|
| Definitely | 2 |
| Somewhat | 2 |
| None / none full survey | 6 |

Interpretation:

- The wake-up problem is not universal in this small sample.
- Orisen should not be marketed as a mass-market alarm clock for everyone at the start.
- The correct wedge is likely people with painful, repeated wake-up failure.
- The `None` respondents are still useful because they help show who is probably not the ICP.
- Do not use these counts as market percentages.

## Repeated problem patterns

### 1. Getting out of bed is often the real problem

Examples from responses:

- “Getting out of bed once awake.”
- “Bad habit when don't have incentive, stay in bed.”
- “Want to wake up earlier.”
- “Gets you out of bed effectively” as ideal alarm-clock language.

Interpretation:

- The pain is often not just hearing the alarm.
- It is the gap between wake-up and actual wake completion.
- This supports Orisen's first wedge: presence-based wake completion.

### 2. Heavy sleepers and multiple alarms are a possible meaningful subsegment

Examples:

- One respondent reported usually taking 7 alarms.
- One respondent said they started sleeping through placing the phone far away.
- Multiple alarms appeared as an existing workaround.

Interpretation:

- A possible subsegment has already tried basic phone-alarm hacks.
- The current solutions do not guarantee wake completion for this group.
- This is a hypothesis, not a validated segment size.

### 3. Phone interaction is a morning failure mode

Examples:

- “Getting off your phone.”
- “Your too smart for it and end up going back to it.”
- “Gets you off your phone” as desired ideal alarm behavior.
- Respondents described time lost, mental health concerns, and bad way to start the day.

Interpretation:

- Phone dependence may be a secondary pain adjacent to wake completion.
- Orisen should not become a generic phone-addiction product, but morning phone avoidance could be part of customer language.

### 4. Motivation and incentive matter, but are not enough alone

Examples:

- “Low incentive.”
- “Incentive not high enough.”
- “Shows you what you need to do for the rest of the day so you're motivated.”
- “Gives you incentive.”

Interpretation:

- Some respondents frame wake-up failure as motivation/incentive, not just alarm failure.
- However, motivation-only solutions may be weak for severe wake-up failure.
- Orisen's stronger angle is not motivational content alone; it is a system that changes the wake-up environment and enforces completion.

### 5. Not enough sleep is a common cause, but does not remove the need

Examples:

- Several respondents connected difficulty waking with less sleep.
- Multiple responses said waking is harder when getting less sleep.

Interpretation:

- Orisen should avoid pretending it replaces enough sleep.
- But the target customer may still need a reliable wake-up system on days when sleep is imperfect.
- This supports careful language: Orisen helps with wake completion; it does not eliminate the biological cost of insufficient sleep.

### 6. Camera discomfort directionally supports non-camera sensing

Question:

- `Which sensors are you uncomfortable having in your alarm clock?`

Observation:

- 7 of 10 respondents mentioned camera discomfort.

Interpretation:

- This directionally supports Orisen's non-camera product direction.
- Non-contact radar is strategically stronger if explained as privacy-preserving, not surveillance-like.
- Do not state this as a market-wide percentage.

## Ideal solution language from respondents

Useful phrases from the raw data:

- “Wakes you up a 100% of the time.”
- “Gets you out of bed effectively.”
- “Gets you off your phone.”
- “Want something else that can wake you up, so you don't have to interact with your phone first thing in the morning.”
- “Shows you what you need to do for the rest of the day so you're motivated.”
- “Gives you incentive.”

How to use this:

- Good for customer-language inspiration.
- Do not reuse “100%” as a public product claim unless carefully qualified.
- Stronger safe wording: “built for people who need more than another phone alarm.”

## Implications for `product/target-customer.md`

The target-customer doc should reflect these as hypotheses and early qualitative patterns, not statistically proven segments.

### Primary ICP hypothesis

People who repeatedly fail between alarm time and actually being out of bed.

### Strong early ICP trait hypotheses

- Sleeps through alarms.
- Sets many alarms.
- Turns alarms off and stays in bed.
- Wakes but does not get out of bed.
- Gets trapped on phone after waking.
- Has recurring weekday or daily wake-up failure.
- Has tried basic hacks such as multiple alarms or placing phone far away.
- Feels real consequences from lost time, missed obligations, money/expenses, productivity, mental health, or relationship friction.

### Not first ICP hypotheses

- People who wake up easily.
- People who say waking is not a meaningful problem.
- People satisfied with their current phone alarm.
- People who only want motivation content.
- People looking for a generic alarm clock.

## Implications for claims

Supported by this interview set:

- Some people experience repeated wake-up and get-out-of-bed problems.
- For some people, basic phone alarms and multiple alarms are not enough.
- Getting out of bed can be a distinct pain from simply waking up.
- Privacy concerns around cameras appeared multiple times in this small sample.
- A solution that wakes people reliably and gets them out of bed is directionally attractive to some respondents.

Not supported by this interview set:

- Broad market demand.
- Willingness to pay.
- Exact price point.
- Production product demand.
- Validated segment ranking by occupation, age, gender, ethnicity, religion, or working status.
- Reduced grogginess.
- Reduced sleep inertia.
- Artificial sleep phase transitioning.
- Radar sleep-stage detection.
- Clinical efficacy.

## Evidence strength

- Evidence category: User interview evidence / early customer discovery.
- Evidence strength: Level 1 to Level 2.
- Confidence: Medium for qualitative themes, low for market sizing, exact ICP proportions, or any subgroup conclusions.

## Limitations

- Small sample size.
- Convenience sampling.
- Mostly younger people.
- Some rows are incomplete.
- Some respondents do not have the problem.
- Some answers are rough notes rather than polished transcripts.
- No payment test.
- No prototype exposure in this interview set.
- No longitudinal behavior measurement.
- Not enough data to compare students vs full-time workers.
- Not enough data to compare men vs women.
- Not enough data to compare by ethnicity, religion, age group, living situation, or accommodation type.
