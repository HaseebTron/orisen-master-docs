This file synthesizes processed external research related to waking up, snoozing, oversleeping, sleep inertia, delayed bed exit, and wake-completion pain.

Use this file to understand what currently processed waking-up sources suggest, what they do not prove, what claim boundaries they imply, and what should be researched or tested next.

## High-level synthesis

The currently processed waking-up sources support a careful version of Orisen's problem framing:

- Many wake-ups are not clean one-step events.
- Snoozing can involve repeated alarm interactions rather than a single alarm response.
- Some people report recurring difficulty with mornings, delayed bed exit, oversleeping, or missed obligations.
- Some snooze users appear to use snooze partly because they worry about failing to wake up on time.
- Sleep inertia is a real research/clinical concept, but Orisen should not publicly claim to reduce it without Orisen-specific testing.
- External research can support the importance of the problem area, but it cannot validate Orisen's product outcomes.

The most useful product framing is not simply:

```text
People need a louder alarm.
```

The better current framing is:

```text
For some people, the failure point is wake completion: the gap between an alarm going off, an alarm being dismissed, and the person actually being out of bed and moving.
```

This framing is supported directionally by the processed sources, especially the repeated snooze behavior documented in the Nature Scientific Reports app-data study, the snooze/sleep-inertia study's finding that some student snooze users wanted reassurance against oversleeping, and the survey sources' reports of delayed bed exit, oversleeping, and missed obligations.

## Main evidence themes

## Theme 1: Snoozing is a repeated wake-up behavior, not just a one-off habit

### Synthesis

The strongest processed source so far is the Nature Scientific Reports study of SleepCycle app users. It found that in this self-selected app-user dataset, many logged sleep sessions ended with snooze use, and snoozing often involved repeated alarm cycles.

This does not prove that snoozing is equally common across the general population. It does show that among engaged users of a sleep/alarm app who used the traditional snooze feature, repeated alarm interaction is a measurable behavior pattern.

The snooze/sleep-inertia study also supports the relevance of snoozing as a behavior pattern, especially among students in its survey sample. It reports high snooze use among surveyed healthy Japanese university students, while the experimental portion was much smaller and should be interpreted cautiously.

### Evidence basis

- `Snooze alarm use in a global population of smartphone users`
- `Effects of using a snooze alarm on sleep inertia after morning awakening`
- Talker Research survey write-up, as weaker directional support around repeated snoozing among alarm users

### Evidence strength

Medium for the idea that repeated snoozing is a real behavior pattern in certain measured or surveyed groups.

Weak to medium for any broad population prevalence claim.

### Orisen implication

Orisen can treat repeated snoozing as a meaningful design target and problem signal.

This supports designing around wake completion rather than only alarm volume, alarm tone, or alarm timing.

### Claim boundary

Do not claim:

- snoozing is harmful in general
- snoozing causes sleep inertia in everyone
- people who snooze are not truly awake
- Orisen reduces snoozing
- Orisen prevents snoozing

Claim-safe direction:

- Some sources show repeated snoozing is common in specific measured or surveyed groups.
- Orisen is designed for people who struggle with repeated alarm dismissal and wake completion.

## Theme 2: Alarm dismissal is not the same as wake completion

### Synthesis

Across the processed sources, a recurring pattern is that waking is not finished when an alarm first sounds or when a user interacts with an alarm.

The Nature app-data study shows repeated snooze interactions across many logged sessions. The Talker Research survey reports delayed bed exit and repeated snoozing among some respondents. The MattressInquirer survey article focuses on oversleeping, wake methods, snoozing, sleeping through alarms, turning alarms off, and missed obligations.

These sources collectively support the conceptual distinction between:

```text
alarm activation
↓
alarm dismissal or snooze
↓
actual wake completion / out-of-bed behavior
```

This distinction is important because Orisen's first wedge is not just “making alarm sound happen.” It is presence-based wake completion.

### Evidence basis

- Nature Scientific Reports app-data study
- Talker Research morning struggles survey write-up
- MattressInquirer oversleeping survey article
- Snooze/sleep-inertia student study, as narrower support around reassurance against oversleeping

### Evidence strength

Medium as a product/problem framing insight.

Weak to medium as public quantitative evidence, because some support comes from self-report survey sources with limited methodology.

### Orisen implication

This is the strongest synthesis theme for Orisen's current wedge.

It supports framing the product around the difference between:

- an alarm being dismissed
- a user actually completing the wake-up by leaving bed

### Claim boundary

Do not claim:

- Orisen guarantees nobody will oversleep
- Orisen has proven wake-completion reliability at production scale
- external research proves presence-based wake completion works

Claim-safe direction:

- Orisen's first wedge is wake completion, not just alarm playback.
- External sources support the idea that some wake-up failures involve repeated snoozing, delayed bed exit, or oversleeping after alarm interaction.

## Theme 3: Oversleeping and delayed bed exit appear meaningful, but current survey evidence is weaker

### Synthesis

The Talker Research and MattressInquirer sources are relevant because they directly discuss morning difficulty, delayed bed exit, oversleeping, alarms, and missed obligations.

Talker Research is cleaner than MattressInquirer as a soft problem-framing source because it reports a 2,000-person U.S. online survey with field dates and sponsor details. It says 38% of respondents agreed they are “bad” at mornings, that respondents get out of bed later than planned multiple times per month on average, and that some alarm users hit snooze repeatedly.

MattressInquirer is more directly related to wake completion because it discusses oversleeping, wake methods, sleeping through alarms, turning alarms off, and missed obligations. However, it is weaker evidence because it uses an MTurk convenience sample, includes respondents as young as 15, lacks field dates, is self-reported, and describes the data as exploratory.

### Evidence basis

- Talker Research / Avocado Green Mattress morning struggles survey write-up
- MattressInquirer oversleeping survey article

### Evidence strength

Medium for Talker Research as directional problem framing.

Weak for MattressInquirer as public-facing prevalence evidence.

Medium internally for identifying problem themes worth testing.

### Orisen implication

These sources support the idea that the waking-up problem has practical consequences beyond annoyance, including delayed starts and missed commitments.

They also help separate Orisen from generic wellness/sleep-tracking language. The target pain is morning failure and wake completion, not only sleep awareness.

### Claim boundary

Do not claim:

- “26% of people oversleep several times a week” as a clean public stat without qualification
- “two in five Americans are bad at mornings” as an unqualified national fact
- survey sources prove broad-market demand or willingness to pay

Claim-safe direction:

- Some survey sources report that a meaningful subset of respondents struggle with mornings, oversleeping, delayed bed exit, or repeated snoozing.
- These sources are useful for directional problem framing, not final proof.

## Theme 4: Sleep inertia is relevant, but Orisen should not claim to reduce it yet

### Synthesis

Sleep inertia is relevant to the waking-up problem because it describes difficulty transitioning from sleep to wakefulness. However, the current processed evidence does not support public claims that Orisen reduces sleep inertia or grogginess.

The snooze/sleep-inertia study gives narrow, cautious evidence that repeated snooze alarms may fragment the final part of sleep and, in a small student lab experiment, were associated with slower later reaction time and worse subjective vigor. But the experiment had only 10 participants, was underpowered, and used a controlled lab setup.

The Trotti sleep inertia / sleep drunkenness review is currently more useful as a claim-control guardrail than as a source for public-facing claims, because the full text was not directly reviewed in the first processing pass.

### Evidence basis

- `Effects of using a snooze alarm on sleep inertia after morning awakening`
- `Waking up is the hardest thing I do all day: Sleep inertia and sleep drunkenness`, only as an incomplete guardrail source

### Evidence strength

Medium for the narrow idea that repeated snooze alarms may fragment the final part of sleep in a small lab context.

Weak for any broad public claim about sleep inertia.

Not sufficient for Orisen efficacy claims.

### Orisen implication

Internally, Orisen should understand sleep inertia and grogginess as relevant to the broader waking-up problem.

Externally, Orisen should avoid making sleep-inertia reduction a proven claim until it has Orisen-specific evidence from real users, controlled tests, or stronger expert-supported validation.

### Claim boundary

Do not claim:

- Orisen reduces sleep inertia
- Orisen reduces grogginess
- Orisen treats sleep drunkenness
- Orisen improves cognitive performance after waking
- getting out of bed fixes sleep inertia

Claim-safe direction:

- Orisen is built for people who struggle with waking up and completing the wake-up process.
- Reduced grogginess and sleep-inertia-related improvements remain hypotheses or product direction unless validated later.

## Theme 5: External sources support problem framing, not Orisen validation

### Synthesis

The processed sources are useful because they establish that wake-up difficulty, repeated snoozing, delayed bed exit, and oversleeping are real enough to investigate.

They do not prove that Orisen's product works.

This distinction matters because external research should shape problem understanding and claim discipline, while Orisen-specific claims require Orisen-specific evidence from prototypes, pilots, user behavior, reliability testing, customer discovery, and eventually transactions or retention.

### Evidence basis

- All processed sources
- `ai-context/claim-control/claim-control-system.md`
- `product/claims-and-evidence.md`

### Evidence strength

Strong as a claim-control principle.

Medium as evidence that waking-up pain is a meaningful problem area.

Weak as evidence for any Orisen-specific outcome.

### Orisen implication

The correct downstream use is:

```text
external research → problem framing and claim discipline
Orisen-specific testing → product claims and validation
```

External research should help Orisen decide what to test, how to describe the problem, and what not to overclaim.

It should not be used to skip validation.

## What this evidence supports

This v1 synthesis supports the following careful conclusions:

- Waking up can be framed as a multi-step process, not only an alarm-sound event.
- Repeated snoozing is a real, observable behavior pattern in at least some measured or surveyed groups.
- Some people report delayed bed exit, oversleeping, repeated snoozing, and missed obligations related to waking up.
- Orisen's focus on wake completion is directionally aligned with problems described in external sources.
- External sources support the need to distinguish alarm dismissal from actual wake completion.
- Sleep inertia and grogginess are relevant concepts, but they require careful handling and Orisen-specific validation before public claims.

## What this evidence does not prove

This evidence does not prove:

- Orisen reduces grogginess.
- Orisen reduces sleep inertia.
- Orisen improves wake-up outcomes in real users.
- Orisen prevents oversleeping.
- Orisen guarantees wake completion.
- Orisen has production reliability.
- Orisen has broad-market demand.
- Orisen has willingness to pay.
- Orisen has clinical efficacy.
- Orisen can accurately detect sleep stages.
- Orisen can deliver validated artificial sleep phase transitioning.

## Claim-relevant conclusions

These conclusions are candidates to flow into `product/claims-and-evidence.md`, after review:

### Safer problem claims

- Some people struggle not only with hearing alarms, but with completing the wake-up process.
- Repeated snoozing, delayed bed exit, and oversleeping appear in multiple external waking-up sources.
- Normal alarms usually end the interaction when the user dismisses or snoozes the alarm; they do not verify wake completion.
- Waking-up failure can have practical consequences, including delayed starts or missed obligations, based on weaker survey evidence.

### Safer Orisen positioning claims

- Orisen is built around wake completion, not just alarm playback.
- Orisen is designed for people whose problem is not merely setting an alarm, but actually getting out of bed.
- Orisen's first wedge is presence-based wake completion.

### Claims requiring caution

- Orisen is designed to make waking up more reliable.
- Orisen is designed to reduce repeated alarm failure.
- Orisen is designed for chronic snoozers or oversleepers.

These may be directionally true as positioning, but need Orisen-specific evidence for stronger wording.

### Claims to avoid

- Orisen reduces sleep inertia.
- Orisen reduces grogginess.
- Orisen wakes users at the perfect sleep stage.
- Orisen prevents oversleeping.
- Orisen guarantees users will never miss an alarm.
- Orisen clinically improves morning alertness.
- Orisen is proven by external sleep research.
