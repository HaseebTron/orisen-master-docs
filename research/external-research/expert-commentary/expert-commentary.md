## Synthesis: Leila Jalali meeting

### Source

Expert:

- Leila Jalali, MD, PhD, CCSH, DBSM.
- Founder at Behavioral Sleep Regenesis.
- Behavioral sleep professional / sleep consultant.
- Background includes sleep health, behavioral sleep medicine, sleep data scoring, sleep clinics, and sleep research.

### Most important takeaways

#### 1. Artificial sleep phase transitioning is plausible but not validated

The strongest technical takeaway is that artificial sleep phase transitioning sounded technically possible to her, but not simple.

She described that disturbance can temporarily move a sleeping person into a lighter stage, but the person may quickly return to the prior sleep stage after about a minute.

Implication:

- Orisen should not frame sleep-stage intervention as a one-time shift.
- If Orisen pursues artificial sleep phase transitioning, it likely needs a continuous, feedback-based intervention loop.
- This supports Orisen's long-term feedback-loop concept directionally, but does not validate that Orisen can do it.

Claim boundary:

- Safe internal framing: technically plausible direction worth testing.
- Unsafe public framing: Orisen can shift users into lighter sleep or control sleep stages.

#### 2. Grogginess and sleep inertia are multi-cause problems

She cautioned that grogginess cannot be eliminated simply by shifting sleep stage.

She described sleep inertia as related partly to waking from deep sleep, but also influenced by sleep quality, REM timing/quantity, deep sleep, and what time the person went to bed.

Implication:

- Orisen should avoid claiming that stage-shifting alone eliminates grogginess or sleep inertia.
- Orisen can pursue less-brutal wake-up as a roadmap goal, but must validate it directly.
- Product and marketing should separate:
  - wake completion, currently strongest evidence
  - easier wake-up / less grogginess, still unvalidated
  - sleep-stage-aware intervention, technical roadmap hypothesis

Claim boundary:

- Safe: grogginess is a problem Orisen aims to reduce over time.
- Careful: waking from lighter stages may help, but outcomes need testing.
- Avoid: Orisen gets rid of grogginess or sleep inertia.

#### 3. Repeated snoozing may be an important intervention target

She said getting rid of repeated snoozing also gets rid of grogginess and inertia.

Interpretation:

- This is highly relevant to Orisen's wake-completion wedge.
- Even before validated sleep-stage intervention, stopping the repeated snooze / return-to-bed loop may be valuable.
- This supports Orisen's behavioral mechanism: keep the user from repeatedly going back to sleep after the alarm.

Claim boundary:

- This supports the hypothesis that preventing snoozing/return-to-bed may improve the morning experience.
- It does not prove Orisen reduces grogginess or inertia without direct Orisen-specific testing.

#### 4. Teens and young adults may be a plausible high-pain segment

She described teens and young adults, roughly ages 14 to 23, as the group that most often struggles with waking up in her experience.

She also connected the issue to screen time, delayed sleep patterns, school/life obligations, and schedule instability.

Implication:

- This supports further investigation of young adults as a target segment.
- It aligns with Orisen's existing early evidence from students and young testers, but does not validate the segment alone.
- The 14 to 23 age range and percentage estimates should be treated as anecdotal/off-the-cuff expert experience, not market data.

Claim boundary:

- Safe: teens/young adults are a segment worth investigating.
- Avoid: most wake-up-problem customers are ages 14 to 23, unless supported by stronger evidence.

#### 5. Shift workers remain a plausible segment, but not first by evidence yet

She identified shift workers as another group that may struggle.

Implication:

- Shift workers are worth researching.
- However, Orisen's current direct evidence is stronger around students/young adults and severe oversleepers than around shift workers.
- Do not pivot to shift workers based only on this expert note.

#### 6. Sleep-stage target direction: N1 and REM

She described N1 and REM as better phases to wake from.

She also noted that a large portion of REM occurs closer to morning and that REM brain activity resembles wakefulness.

Implication:

- This directionally supports the idea of timing/intervention around lighter/REM-like phases.
- It does not validate consumer-grade stage detection or Orisen's ability to intervene correctly.

#### 7. Sleep spindles may matter

She suggested looking into sleep spindles and noted that if someone has spindles, it can be hard for them to wake up.

Implication:

- Add sleep spindles to the future research list.
- This may matter for understanding arousal thresholds and wake difficulty.
- It is not yet an Orisen feature or claim.

## Implications for product strategy

### Reinforces the current product hierarchy

The expert commentary supports this hierarchy:

1. Wake completion: strongest current evidence and safest first wedge.
2. Reduce repeated snoozing / return-to-bed behavior: strong behavioral target.
3. Easier wake-up / reduced grogginess: important user-desired outcome but still unvalidated.
4. Artificial sleep phase transitioning: plausible long-term technical direction requiring feedback-based validation.

### Reinforces continuous feedback loop concept

If intervention is pursued, it likely cannot be a one-time event.

This supports Orisen's concept as a sensing + feedback + intervention system, not simply an alarm clock.

### Reinforces claim discipline

This commentary is a warning against overclaiming:

- “fixes sleep”
- “eliminates grogginess”
- “controls sleep stages”
- “wakes you from the perfect stage”
- “clinically proven”

## Implications for target customer

This expert note supports investigating:

- teens and young adults with delayed sleep patterns
- students with screen-time-heavy nights and irregular schedules
- severe oversleepers
- people who repeatedly snooze or return to bed
- shift workers

But it does not prove any of these as the final ICP.

The current primary ICP should still be framed behaviorally:

- people who repeatedly fail between alarm time and actually being out of bed

Rather than demographically:

- students
- teens
- boys
- shift workers

## Implications for evidence and validation

Future validation should test:

- whether Orisen reduces wake-completion failure
- whether Orisen reduces repeated snoozing or return-to-bed events
- whether Orisen reduces self-reported grogginess or sleep inertia
- whether continuous audio/light intervention can keep users in lighter stages near wake time
- whether radar-derived signals are accurate enough for feedback-based intervention
- whether users tolerate continuous/intermittent intervention while sleeping
- whether outcomes persist beyond novelty

## Claims supported by this commentary
