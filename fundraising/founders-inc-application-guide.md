## Purpose

This file is a general rubric for applying to Founders, Inc.

It explains what Founders, Inc appears to look for, what signals the application questions are testing, and what kind of answer is likely strongest for each question.

This file should not contain company-specific application answers.

A separate company-specific application draft can be created later and should use this guide as an input.

## Source basis

This guide is based on:

- Founders, Inc's public About page.
- Founders, Inc's public Off Season II page.
- Founders, Inc's public Campus page.
- General startup-program application reasoning.

Primary public signals from Founders, Inc:

- They describe themselves as backing founders with "vision and grit."
- They mostly invest in emerging technology and industries.
- Listed domains include AR/VR and gaming, hardware, AI/ML, web3, dev/tools, and consumer.
- They describe good-fit founders as prolific at building, technical and curious, chasing emerging tech, having founder x market fit, having a unique point of view, showing ambition and drive, and having a generous attitude.
- They say applicants should apply when they have a strong point of view on what they are building, have shown they can build something big, have built cool projects or traction, have a clear plan for capital, are deep in their problem space, and have made moves to build motion.
- Off Season II is framed as six weeks of focused building with no curriculum, no distractions, and a demo day.
- Their campus emphasizes physical proximity to strong builders, hardware resources, media resources, office hours, founder events, and a high bar for access.

## Overall interpretation

Founders, Inc is not primarily evaluating a polished pitch-deck narrative.

The main application question is:

> Is this person an unusually intense builder with a real point of view, enough technical ability to ship, founder-market fit, and the ability to create visible momentum during the program?

The application should feel like a high-signal founder profile, not a corporate pitch.

## Summary of what they are looking for

A clean version of the rubric:

- Ambition, drive, vision, and grit.
- Obsession with a real problem.
- Prolific builder energy.
- Technical ability or technical depth sufficient to ship.
- Curiosity and comfort with emerging technology.
- Founder-market fit.
- Unique point of view.
- Visible motion or traction.
- Ability to create momentum during the cohort.
- Community fit and generous attitude.

Suggested interpretation:

- Merge "ambition and drive" with "vision and grit" into one founder-intensity bucket.
- Keep "chasing emerging tech" separate from vision and grit. It is more about category fit and curiosity.
- Keep "prolific at building" separate from pure technical ability. A person can be technical but not prolific, or prolific but not classically technical.
- Treat "generous attitude" as a campus/community-fit signal, not the main startup-quality signal.
- Keep "obsessed with a real problem" as its own signal because it affects almost every answer.

Answer style principles:

- Specific beats impressive.
- Concrete proof beats adjectives.
- Builder evidence beats market narrative.
- Honest stage beats inflated traction.
- Clear next milestone beats vague ambition.
- Founder insight beats generic category language.
- Real demo beats polished deck.

Answer-review checklist:

- The founder is actively building now.
- The problem is specific and painful.
- The founder has a non-obvious point of view.
- The founder has enough technical ability to ship.
- There is some visible motion or evidence.
- The August 7 milestone is concrete and believable.
- The application explains why Founders, Inc is the right environment now.
- The answer does not overstate traction, technical proof, or product readiness.
- The answer does not sound like a generic accelerator application.

## Core signals and how to show them

- **Ambition, drive, vision, and grit**
  - What they want:
    - Founders who sound like they are going to build regardless of the program.
  - Show this through:
    - A large but specific ambition.
    - A clear reason the problem matters.
    - Evidence of work already done.
    - Willingness to use the cohort to compress time and create momentum.
  - Avoid:
    - Generic ambition.
    - Overly polished language without proof.
    - Framing the program as something needed before starting.

- **Obsession with a real problem**
  - What they want:
    - A founder who is deep in the problem space.
  - Show this through:
    - Specific customer or user pain.
    - A clear reason current solutions fail.
    - Evidence from user conversations, usage, personal experience, or repeated observation.
    - A non-obvious insight about the problem.
  - Avoid:
    - Starting with market size.
    - Describing a nice-to-have convenience.
    - Sounding like the founder picked the idea because the category is trendy.

- **Prolific builder energy**
  - What they want:
    - Evidence that the founder builds a lot, not just thinks or talks.
  - Show this through:
    - Past projects.
    - Current working prototypes.
    - Demos.
    - Repos.
    - Technical artifacts.
    - User tests.
    - Shipped experiments.
  - Avoid:
    - Talking only about plans.
    - Treating a landing page as the main proof of building if the product is technical.
    - Saying "we are exploring" without showing what has already been made.

- **Technical ability sufficient to ship**
  - What they want:
    - Confidence that the founder can create product progress without waiting for someone else.
  - Show this through:
    - Specific technical skills.
    - Past technical builds.
    - Current technical ownership.
    - Ability to prototype, test, debug, and iterate.
    - Honest explanation of what the founder can build personally versus what requires teammates, contractors, or future hires.
  - Avoid:
    - Inflating technical ability.
    - Sounding dependent on hiring before the core product can move.
    - Saying "I am non-technical" without a compensating builder advantage.

- **Curiosity and emerging-technology fit**
  - What they want:
    - Founders working near emerging technology, with curiosity and technical taste.
  - Show this through:
    - A product that uses or creates new technical capability.
    - A founder who is actively learning and experimenting.
    - A clear reason the company belongs in a builder-heavy emerging-tech environment.
  - Avoid:
    - Buzzword stuffing.
    - Using AI, hardware, robotics, or other categories as labels without explaining what is technically interesting.

- **Founder-market fit**
  - What they want:
    - A reason this founder is unusually suited to this problem.
  - Show this through:
    - Relevant background.
    - Lived experience or deep exposure.
    - Technical skill fit.
    - Network or customer access.
    - Prior projects in the domain.
    - A personal or professional reason the founder will keep going when it gets hard.
  - Avoid:
    - Claiming passion without evidence.
    - Describing the market as attractive but not explaining why this founder is the right person.

- **Unique point of view**
  - What they want:
    - A founder with a thesis, not just a product idea.
  - Show this through:
    - A clear belief about how the world is changing.
    - A sharp critique of current solutions.
    - A non-obvious product insight.
    - A reason the product should exist now.
  - Avoid:
    - Safe, generic category language.
    - Copying common startup phrasing.
    - Trying to sound like every other startup in the space.

- **Visible motion or traction**
  - What they want:
    - Signs that the founder has already created energy around the idea.
  - Show this through:
    - Users.
    - Waitlist.
    - Revenue.
    - Pilots.
    - Strong demo response.
    - Social traction.
    - Community interest.
    - Customer discovery volume.
    - Prototype usage.
    - Distribution experiments.
  - Avoid:
    - Overstating what the traction proves.
    - Mixing up interest, signups, pilots, revenue, and retention.
    - Hiding weak traction behind vague phrases like "strong interest."

- **Ability to create momentum during the cohort**
  - What they want:
    - A clear sense of what will materially change by demo day.
  - Show this through:
    - A concrete ship goal.
    - A narrow scope.
    - A visible demo milestone.
    - A test plan.
    - A user or traction milestone.
    - Evidence the founder knows what to prioritize.
  - Avoid:
    - Vague goals like "improve the product."
    - Goals that are too broad for the program window.
    - Goals that depend entirely on external parties.

- **Community fit and generous attitude**
  - What they want:
    - A founder who contributes to the campus/community instead of only extracting from it.
  - Show this through:
    - Willingness to help other builders.
    - High-energy participation.
    - Clear interest in working around intense founders.
    - A collaborative attitude without sounding unfocused.
  - Avoid:
    - Treating the program only as funding.
    - Sounding extractive.
    - Sounding like the founder wants status more than building time.

## Application question rubric

- **What’s your name?**
  - What this tests:
    - Basic identity.
  - Best answer type:
    - Simple, exact, no extra framing.

- **Email**
  - What this tests:
    - Contact reliability.
  - Best answer type:
    - Use the email the founder actually checks.
    - Prefer a professional or company email if it is reliable.

- **Where are you based?**
  - What this tests:
    - Geography.
    - Relocation or campus logistics.
    - Local ecosystem context.
  - Best answer type:
    - City, region, country.
    - Mention willingness or ability to be in San Francisco only if true and relevant.

- **Are you applying as a solo founder or do you have a team?**
  - What this tests:
    - Team risk.
    - Execution capacity.
    - Founder ownership.
  - Best answer type:
    - Be direct.
    - If solo, state it confidently and show how execution risk is handled.
    - If team, explain who owns what and why the team can move fast.
  - Avoid:
    - Sounding apologetic for being solo.
    - Describing unclear or casual collaborators as core team members.

- **Drop a 30-60 second intro video**
  - Prompt details:
    - "Don't overthink it."
    - Tell them who you are, what you are building, and why you applied.
    - Help them put a face to the name.
  - What this tests:
    - Founder energy.
    - Clarity.
    - Communication ability.
    - Authenticity.
    - Whether the founder feels like a real builder, not just a polished applicant.
  - Best answer type:
    - Speak directly to camera.
    - Start with who you are.
    - Say what you are building in plain language.
    - Say why you applied to Founders, Inc specifically.
    - Briefly show founder-market fit or builder intensity if it fits naturally.
    - Keep it human and clear rather than scripted.
  - Suggested structure:
    - 5-10 seconds: who you are.
    - 10-20 seconds: what you are building.
    - 10-20 seconds: why the problem matters or why you are the right founder.
    - 10-15 seconds: why Founders, Inc is the right environment now.
  - Avoid:
    - Trying to fit the entire pitch into the video.
    - Reading a script word-for-word.
    - Sounding like a corporate pitch deck.
    - Overexplaining roadmap, market size, or technical details.

- **Which category best fits what you’re building?**
  - What this tests:
    - Fit with Founders, Inc's emerging-tech focus.
    - Whether the founder understands the product category.
  - Best answer type:
    - Pick the most accurate primary category.
    - Use secondary categories only if the application allows it.
    - Do not force trendy categories if they are not central to the product.

- **What are you building right now?**
  - Prompt details:
    - One line.
    - Be concrete.
    - 80 characters.
  - What this tests:
    - Whether the founder is actively building.
    - Product clarity.
    - Current stage.
    - Whether the founder can compress the product into a simple, concrete line.
  - Best answer type:
    - One short line under 80 characters.
    - Present tense.
    - Concrete product, user, and outcome if possible.
    - Emphasize what is being built now, not only the long-term vision.
  - Strong answer pattern:
    - "A [product] for [specific user] that [specific outcome]."
  - Avoid:
    - A giant vision statement.
    - Market-size framing.
    - Buzzwords without a concrete behavior.
    - Claims that imply unvalidated capabilities are already proven.
    - Saying only what the company will eventually become.

- **Company name**
  - What this tests:
    - Basic company identity.
  - Best answer type:
    - Company name only, unless a short clarification is necessary.

- **What does it do, and who is it for?**
  - What this tests:
    - Problem clarity.
    - Customer clarity.
    - Product wedge.
    - Whether the founder can explain the company simply.
  - Best answer type:
    - One sentence for what it does.
    - One sentence for who it is for.
    - Use plain language.
    - Focus on a painful early customer, not everyone.
  - Avoid:
    - Generic category language.
    - A broad target market.
    - Feature lists without a sharp customer pain.

- **Link to your product / project**
  - What this tests:
    - Proof of existence.
    - Public-facing seriousness.
    - Ability to show work.
  - Best answer type:
    - Website, demo page, GitHub, portfolio, or product link.
    - The link should make the project look alive and coherent.

- **Product demo**
  - Prompt details:
    - Drop a Loom or YouTube link.
    - A phone video is fine.
    - It does not have to be polished.
    - They want to see what progress has been made.
  - What this tests:
    - Whether there is something real.
    - Builder proof.
    - Ability to communicate product progress visually.
    - Whether the founder can show working behavior instead of only describing the idea.
  - Best answer type:
    - Show the core product behavior.
    - Prefer real demo over polished mockup.
    - Show what works today.
    - Use a simple Loom, YouTube, or phone-recorded demo.
    - If unfinished, clearly label what is prototype, simulated, stubbed, or planned.
  - Avoid:
    - Only a pitch deck.
    - Only rendered product visuals.
    - A demo that hides the actual product loop.
    - Overproducing the demo instead of showing real progress.
    - Implying unvalidated capabilities are already proven unless the demo actually supports them.

- **What is the coolest technical thing you’ve built?**
  - What this tests:
    - Technical spike.
    - Builder credibility.
    - Taste in hard problems.
    - Whether the founder has done unusually difficult work.
  - Best answer type:
    - Pick one thing.
    - Explain what made it technically hard.
    - Explain what the founder personally built.
    - Mention the result, artifact, or proof.
  - Avoid:
    - Listing many projects without depth.
    - Picking the most prestigious project if it was not personally owned.
    - Describing teamwork vaguely without founder contribution.

- **What are you the top 1% in the world at?**
  - What this tests:
    - Founder spike.
    - Self-awareness.
    - Differentiation.
    - Confidence.
  - Best answer type:
    - Choose a specific, credible edge.
    - Connect it to the founder's ability to build the company.
    - Make it unusual enough to be memorable.
  - Avoid:
    - Generic traits like hardworking, passionate, disciplined, or fast learner.
    - Claims that sound fake or impossible to verify.
    - Humble non-answers.

- **Are you technical?**
  - What this tests:
    - Execution ability.
    - Product-building independence.
  - Best answer type:
    - Answer directly.
    - Give technical domains, tools, and examples.
    - Be honest about depth.
  - Avoid:
    - Saying yes without evidence.
    - Overexplaining weaknesses.

- **Can you personally build and ship the core product without outside help?**
  - What this tests:
    - Dependency risk.
    - Founder execution capacity.
    - Whether progress stops without a hire.
  - Best answer type:
    - Be honest but confident.
    - Separate prototype or MVP ability from production-scale ability.
    - Show that the founder can personally drive the core product forward.
  - Avoid:
    - Saying no without a mitigation plan.
    - Pretending to be able to do everything if that is not true.
    - Making the company sound blocked until a cofounder appears.

- **GitHub**
  - What this tests:
    - Technical proof.
    - Shipping history.
    - Code or documentation artifacts.
  - Best answer type:
    - Provide the strongest relevant GitHub profile or repo.
    - If important work is private, say access can be shared or point to a demo.

- **Do you have users?**
  - What this tests:
    - Real demand.
    - Traction.
    - Customer validation.
    - Whether the founder has left idea-land.
  - Best answer type:
    - Be precise.
    - Separate active users, testers, pilots, waitlist, paying customers, revenue, and customer interviews.
    - Say what the evidence proves and what it does not yet prove.
  - Avoid:
    - Inflating waitlist into users.
    - Calling verbal interest traction without context.
    - Hiding lack of users behind vague excitement.

- **What do you want to ship by August 7th?**
  - What this tests:
    - Program fit.
    - Prioritization.
    - Execution plan.
    - Whether the founder can create momentum inside the cohort window.
  - Best answer type:
    - State a concrete demo-day milestone.
    - Include the product behavior that will work.
    - Include a user or traction milestone if realistic.
    - Keep the scope focused enough to be believable.
  - Avoid:
    - Vague goals.
    - Too many milestones.
    - Goals that depend on external approvals or long sales cycles.

- **Do you want to be considered for the in-person SF campus cohort?**
  - What this tests:
    - Commitment.
    - Ability to benefit from the campus.
    - Logistics.
  - Best answer type:
    - If yes, answer clearly and enthusiastically.
    - If no, be honest and do not fake availability.

- **Any other ideas?**
  - What this tests:
    - Creativity.
    - Focus.
    - Whether the founder is scattered.
  - Best answer type:
    - Usually keep the focus on the main company.
    - Mention adjacent ideas only if they reinforce the founder's domain obsession or future roadmap.
  - Avoid:
    - Listing unrelated startup ideas.
    - Sounding unfocused.

- **Are you a student?**
  - What this tests:
    - Stage.
    - Availability.
    - Context.
  - Best answer type:
    - Direct yes or no.
    - If recently graduated or leaving school, state that clearly.

- **Have you raised any funding?**
  - What this tests:
    - Funding stage.
    - Investor validation.
    - Capital needs.
  - Best answer type:
    - Direct and precise.
    - Include amount, source, and structure only if relevant.
    - If none, say none confidently.
  - Avoid:
    - Making unfunded sound like failure.
    - Describing grants, prizes, or non-dilutive support unclearly.

- **LinkedIn**
  - What this tests:
    - Public credibility.
    - Background.
    - Network.
    - Professional proof.
  - Best answer type:
    - Provide profile link.
    - Make sure the profile is consistent with the application.

- **Twitter / X**
  - What this tests:
    - Builder-in-public signal.
    - Distribution potential.
    - Founder voice.
    - Public motion.
  - Best answer type:
    - Provide the profile if it helps.
    - A small but active account can be better than no public presence if it shows real building.

- **Personal site / portfolio**
  - What this tests:
    - Builder proof.
    - Past work.
    - Taste.
    - Ability to communicate technical projects.
  - Best answer type:
    - Link to the clearest proof of past builds.
    - The portfolio should make the founder's spike obvious.

- **What made you decide to apply?**
  - What this tests:
    - Fit with Founders, Inc specifically.
    - Whether the founder understands the program.
    - Whether the founder wants to build or just wants status or funding.
  - Best answer type:
    - Mention the exact reason this environment helps now.
    - Emphasize building, speed, accountability, community, and demo-day pressure.
    - Explain why the timing matters.
  - Avoid:
    - Generic accelerator language.
    - Saying only "funding."
    - Sounding like the founder wants permission or validation.

- **How did you hear about Founders, Inc?**
  - What this tests:
    - Attribution.
    - Network path.
    - Whether the founder is already in relevant circles.
  - Best answer type:
    - Be honest.
    - If through a specific person, post, event, or founder, say so.

- **Did someone refer you?**
  - What this tests:
    - Warm signal.
    - Network credibility.
  - Best answer type:
    - If yes, name the person and relationship.
    - If no, say no.
  - Avoid:
    - Stretching a weak connection into a referral.
